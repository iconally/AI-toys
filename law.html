Is Your Child’s AI Toy Actually Safe?

In 2024, the European Union (EU) adopted the world’s first comprehensive law on Artificial Intelligence, called the “AI Act”. Because of that, the EU is widely seen as one of the most advanced regulators of Artificial Intelligence in the world, and together with strict toy-safety and cybersecurity rules, the EU offers children among the most far-reaching legal protection around children’s toys in the world. However, the legal situation is not perfect, and parents should not assume that every toy on the European market is automatically perfectly safe — especially if the toy in question is a product of fast-moving AI technologies.

By staying informed about how AI toys work and making careful choices, parents can and should add an important extra layer of protection for their children.

EU

EU Regulation on Toy Safety: Status Quo

Since 2009, EU toy-safety law was based on rules written long before Artificial Intelligence became a feature one could find in children’s toys. Those old rules focused on the kinds of dangers parents have always worried about (toxic chemicals, sharp edges, choking hazards), but did not address the topic of AI and internet-connected toys.

Only very recently, on 12 December 2025, the EU replaced the outdated law with a new, 85-page updated regulation called Regulation (EU) 2025/2509 on the Safety of Toys. All of the classic safety checks still apply in an updated form, but the new rules go further, because today’s toys require stricter regulation.

EU

Scan a Code, Know What’s Inside

The new Regulation (EU) 2025/2509 on the Safety of Toys requires that AI-powered toys comply with the AI Act (including cybersecurity, personal data protection, and privacy requirements). As a result, certain AI toys which may encourage dangerous behaviour in children will lead to complete bans on the European market. The new regulation also tightens obligations for online sales and imports, aiming to catch unsafe toys that enter the EU market through online platforms or from outside the EU.

One of the most practical changes for parents is something called the Digital Product Passport. This means that every toy sold in the EU will have to come with extensive documentation which can be easily accessed. To access it, parents will simply have to scan a code (like a QR code) on the toy or its packaging. In seconds, key information about what the toy is made of, whether it meets EU safety standards, and any important warnings will be available — all in a clear, digital format.

The passport isn’t just useful for parents. It also gives customs officials and regulators a faster, more reliable way to check whether toys made outside the EU are actually safe and legal before they ever reach the European shop shelves.

EU

The New Law Exists, But 2030 Is Still Far Away

By passing the new Regulation on the Safety of Toys, the EU positions itself as the world’s strictest standard for chemical toxicity and physical safety in toys. However, even though the new regulation formally entered into force on 1 January 2026, it will only start applying after a transition period of four and a half years, on 1 August 2030.

What does this mean for children and parents? In practice, the old rules from the outdated directive still govern most toys on the market today, while the new, stronger protections are only slowly being put in place. In other words, even though the new regulation entered into force a few months ago, it will take a few years before most of its requirements actually apply. Authorities and manufacturers are using this period to prepare systems such as the above-mentioned Digital Product Passport and new testing procedures — but children are already playing with internet-connected and AI-enabled toys now.

For parents, the key message is this: the EU law is moving in the right direction, yet there is still a gap between future legal protections and the everyday risks that current toys may pose to children’s wellbeing.
