Are AI toys safe?

Despite (some) companies adding guardrails, multiple reports show that they can break down (for examples see the Trouble in Toyland 2025 report and the Common Sense Media assessment). Reports show that some AI toys can move into inappropriate conversation topics, including explicit sexual content, use emotional manipulation techniques, fail to recognise risky behaviour, and use child-inappropriate language. The Trouble in Toyland 2025 report further highlights that internet-connected AI toys are at risk of being hacked, allowing attackers to eavesdrop on your family and even interact with your children through the toys. Your sensitive data may even be stolen from the companies when they get hacked.

Do AI toys help children grow up?

Research by Xu and He (2026) shows that children interact differently with AI toys than with humans. Often these interactions are less rich and varied, which is why researchers and specialists worry that long-term AI toy use can have negative effects on social and cognitive development in children. There are concerns that AI toys which act as a ‘best friend’ can cause emotional dependence. However, these concerns remain unconfirmed, because the AI toy industry has only recently entered the market and long-term research has not yet caught up.

Can AI toys help children learn?

When it comes to learning a new language or specific subject-related knowledge, AI technology may support a child’s learning process. However, AI cannot replace the importance of learning from other humans. AI toys can stimulate curiosity, creativity, and self-regulation skills, but these findings are only based on momentary, short-term observations. If you want an AI toy to support your child’s learning, make sure it was specifically built for that purpose, and that the manufacturer is transparent about how the toy works. You can find more information on the educational possibilities of AI toys in the report by Xu and He (2026).

How do I introduce AI toys to my child?

Start with an honest, age-appropriate explanation. You do not need to explain how AI works in technical terms. For young children, saying ‘this is a robot toy that can talk and respond to you, but it is not alive and it does not have feelings’ is a good starting point. Older children can handle more detail about what AI actually is and how it differs from other toys and from humans.

Set boundaries from the beginning. Decide in advance when and how long your child can use the AI toy, and stick to it. Predictable, limited use is one of the most effective ways to prevent emotional dependence.

Watch for signs of unhealthy attachment. It is normal for children to enjoy playing with an AI toy and to start treating toys as friends. However, you should be concerned if your child is distressed when separated from it, prefers the AI toy over human interaction, or seems confused about whether the toy is alive. If you notice these signs, gently decrease the toy’s presence, replace it with traditional toys, talk with your child, and seek professional help if you conclude that you need expert guidance.

Keep the conversation going as your child grows. A five-year-old and a ten-year-old must be approached differently. Revisit the important topics as your child matures, explaining that AI toys are designed to seem friendly, but that this is different from a real human-to-human friendship. Decide on appropriate explanations depending on your child’s age.

AI toys work best alongside human interaction, not instead of it. Make sure your child has plenty of play, conversation, and learning time with other people and children. An AI toy can be a fun addition to your child’s life, but it should not in any way be a substitute for human relationships.

I still can’t decide.

Read the sources we link to and weigh the risks against the benefits. The most important rule, if you decide to buy an AI toy, is to make sure the toy is safe and never replaces human interaction. No matter how engaging the toy is, your child’s most important relationships and learning experiences should always come from the people around them.
